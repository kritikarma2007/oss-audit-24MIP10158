  Every day, I rely on python — a piece of software that
  someone built, tested, and shared with the world at no
  cost and with no restrictions. That act of sharing is
  not just generosity. It is a statement about how
  knowledge should move through the world.

  To me, freedom means Choice. In the context of software,
  that freedom is not abstract. It means I can read the
  code that powers my tools. I can understand it, question
  it, and change it. A program I cannot read is a program
  I have not truly been given — I have only been lent it,
  on terms I did not set and cannot change.

  I commit that when I build A learning app, I will share it.
  Not because I am required to. Not because it earns me
  credit. But because the tools I used to build it were
  shared with me by people I will never meet, and the
  only honest response to that gift is to pass it forward.

  Open source is not a license. It is a philosophy.
  It says that what we build together is stronger than
  what any one of us could build alone — and that the
  measure of a tool is not what it costs, but what it
  enables others to do.
